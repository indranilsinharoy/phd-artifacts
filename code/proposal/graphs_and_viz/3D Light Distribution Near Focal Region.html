Introductory section

Fresnel Number (\(N_F\))

Definition

The Fresnel number (\(N_F\)) is given as:

\[N_F = \frac{a^2}{\lambda z}\]

where, \(a\) is the radius of the circular aperture, \(\lambda\) is the wavelength, and \(z\) is the distance of the observation plane from the aperture.

From the Huygens-Fresnel principle perspective, the Fresnel number, N, represents the number of Fresnel zones in the aperture opening [Born and Wolf 2011].

Relation to three dimensional light distribution near the focal region
  • Fresnel numbers of conventional imaging systems are very large compared to unity (\(N_F \gg 1\)). Under such circumstances, the Debye representation of the focused field is a very good approximation [Wolf 2001].

The impulse response of a positive lens

We start our derivation, from the equation that represents the impulse response of a positive lens [Goodman 2005, equation 5-28]:
Note: We ignore the quadratic phase factor that is in terms of the image coordinates as we are interested in the intensities.

Assumptions:

  1. The following derivation uses the Fresnel diffraction formula which incorporates the following assumptions:
    1. Scalar diffraction theory
    2. The observation distance is much greater than \(\lambda\)
    3. Observation point is very close to the optical axis (paraxial assumption)
  2. Also, the lens is assumed to be "thin lens", and paraxial assumptions are used for the derivation of the transfer function of the thin lens. (A consequence of this assumption is that the spherical surfaces of the lens are approximated by parabolic surfaces) [Goodman 2005].

Also, since we are interested in the amplitude and intensity distribution at the focal plane due to a point source, we shall represent this distribution as the impulse response "\(h()\)"

\[ h(u,v,z_i;\xi,\eta,z_o) = -\frac{1}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} e^{jk z_i} e^{j\frac{k}{2 z_o}\left( \xi^2 + \eta^2 \right)}e^{j\frac{k}{2 z_i} (u^2 + v^2)} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} {P(x,y){e^{j k \frac{1}{2}\left( {\frac{1}{{{z_o}}} + \frac{1}{{{z_i}}} - \frac{1}{f}} \right)\left( {{x^2} + {y^2}} \right)}}{e^{ - j\frac{k}{{{z_i}}}\big[ {(u - M \xi) x + (v - M \eta) y} \big]}}dxdy}\]

in the above expression, \(M = - \frac{z_i}{z_o}\)

Using a series of approximations as described in section 5.3.2 in [Goodman 2005], and the fact that we are (or eventually will be) interested in the intensity of the spread function, we can write the above equation as:

\[ h(u, v, z_i; \xi, \eta, z_o) \approx \frac{1}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} {P(x,y){e^{j k \frac{1}{2}\left( {\frac{1}{{{z_o}}} + \frac{1}{{{z_i}}} - \frac{1}{f}} \right)\left( {{x^2} + {y^2}} \right)}}{e^{ - j\frac{k}{{{z_i}}}\big[ {(u - M \xi) x + (v - M \eta) y} \big]}}dxdy} \]

"Thus if the lens law is satisfied, the impulse response is seen to be given (up to an extra scaling factor \(1/\lambda z_o\)) by the Fraunhofer diffraction pattern of the lens aperture, centered on the image coordinates (\(u = M\xi, v = M\eta\))" [Goodman 2005]

The above equation is space invariant (due to magnification and inversion). In order to convert the form of the above equatioin so that it may become a "convolution-type", we make the following substitution:

Let \(\bar{\xi} =M\xi\) and \(\bar{\eta} =M\eta\).

The above substitution essentially takes care of the magnification and the image inversion, giving us a "reduced cooridnate system" of the object space. In terms of (code) implementation, this step essentially means that you need to first apply "geometric imaging" to the object, in which we get an perfect, scaled, inverted image, and then apply the convolution kernel (if the PSF is space invariant) to the geometric image in this reduced coordinate system.

The transfer functions becomes

\[ h(u, v, z_i; \bar{\xi}, \bar{\eta}, z_o) \approx \frac{1}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} {P(x,y){e^{j k \frac{1}{2}\left( {\frac{1}{{{z_o}}} + \frac{1}{{{z_i}}} - \frac{1}{f}} \right)\left( {{x^2} + {y^2}} \right)}}{e^{ - j\frac{k}{{{z_i}}}\big[ {(u - \bar{\xi}) x + (v - \bar{\eta}) y} \big]}}dxdy} \]

which is function of the difference of coordinates. Therefore, \(h(u, v, z_i; \bar{\xi}, \bar{\eta}, z_o) = h(u - \bar{\xi}, v - \bar{\eta},z_i,z_o)\)

Since the above impulse response is a function of only the difference of object coordinates (in the reduced object coordinates), the impulse response can be written as:

\[ h(u, v, z_i, z_o) = \frac{1}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} {P(x,y){e^{j k \frac{1}{2}\left( {\frac{1}{{{z_o}}} + \frac{1}{{{z_i}}} - \frac{1}{f}} \right)\left( {{x^2} + {y^2}} \right)}}{e^{ - j\frac{k}{{{z_i}}}\left( {ux + vy} \right)}}dxdy}\]

The imaging equation can be represented as follows:

\[ U_i(u,v, z_i, z_o) = \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{|M|}h(u - \bar{\xi}, v - \bar{\eta},z_i,z_o) \left[ \frac{1}{|M|} U_o \Big( \frac{\bar{\xi} }{M},\frac{\bar{\eta}}{M} \Big) \right] d\bar{\xi} d \bar{\eta} \]

For a discussion about the effect of the quadratic phase factors in terms of the object coordinates, and the effects of magnification, see section 5.3.2 of Goodman. Also, the effect of magnification is that the impulse response is in general space variant.

The above expression may be written in terms of the defocus aberration coefficient defined as follows:

Let \(z_p\) is the distance from the pupil to the point where the perfect image (diffraction limited) of the point object is formed and the actual distance of the image plane from the pupil is \(z_i\) (In Goodman and many other books, \(z_i\) is represented by \(z_a\), where \(a\) is used to represent the actual or aberrated wavefront).

Also, let

\[z_i = z_p + \delta z\]

such that, for negative focus shifts (i.e. for \(\delta z < 0\)), \(z_i < z_p\) (and the aberrated wavefront is to the right of the reference wavefront), and let

\[\left(\frac{1}{z_o}+\frac{1}{z_i}-\frac{1}{f}\right) = - W_{d} \hspace{2 mm}\]

such that if \(z_i=z_p \hspace{1 mm}\) (image plane at geometrical focus), then \(\left(\frac{1}{z_o}+\frac{1}{z_p}-\frac{1}{f}\right)=0\)

Then,

\[ W_d = \left(\frac{1}{z_p} - \frac{1}{z_i} \right) = \frac{z_i - z_p}{z_i z_p} = \frac{\delta z}{z_i z_p} = \frac{\delta z}{z_p (z_p + \delta z)} \]

Note:

The reason for \(-W_d\) instead of just \(W_d\) in the above expression is mainly because of the following two reasons:

  1. Doing so, we get a very similar expression, in polar coordinates, for the distribution of complex field near the vicinity of the focal point as shown in [Born and Wolf 2011]
  2. If we define \(\delta z = z_i - z_p\), and also choose positive \(W_d\), then we get \(W_d = - \delta z/{z_p z_i}\). This eventually leads to \(u = -k \delta z /{4 N^2}\)...this might be a bit strange.

The optical path length error (or Optical Path Difference, OPD), \(W(x,y)\) is given as difference between the aberrated (\(W_2(x,y)\)) and the un-aberrated wavefront (\(W_p(x,y)\)):

\(W(x, y) = W_2(x,y) - W_p(x,y)\)

\(W(x,y) = -\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{z_i} - \frac{1}{z_p}\right)(x^2 + y^2) = \frac{W_d}{2} (x^2 + y^2)\)

The generalized pupil is given as as:

\(\mathcal{P}(x,y) = P(x,y)e^{jkW(x,y)}\)

or

\(\mathcal{P}(x,y) = P(x,y)e^{jk \frac{W_d}{2} (x^2 + y^2)}\)

The above is the equation for the generalized coherent impulse response if the only aberration present is defocus.

[Aside: Relation of \(W_d\) to the wavefront defocus coefficient \(W_{020}\)]

\[W(x,y) = \frac{W_d}{2}(x^2 + y^2) = \frac{\delta z}{2 z_p z_i} (x^2 + y^2)\]

if \(a\) is the radius of the aperture, then let \(x = a \rho cos \theta\) and \(y = a \rho sin \theta\). Then,

\[W(x,y) = \frac{W_d}{2}a^2 \rho ^2 = \frac{\delta z}{2 z_p z_i} a^2 \rho ^2 = W_{020} \rho^2\]

\[ \boxed{ W(x,y) = W_{020} \rho^2 } \]

\[\boxed{ W_{020} = \frac{\delta z \, a^2}{2 z_p (z_p + \delta z)} \approx \frac{\delta z}{8 N^2} } \]

Where, \(W_{020}\) is the maximum wavefront error measured at the edge of the pupil (\(\rho = 1\)), \(N\) is the f-number.

Note:

The sign convensions in the above relations between the path length error \(W(x,y)\) and position of the aberrated wavefront with respect to the reference wavefront conforms to the convension of [Goodman 2005]. For convenience, the statement from [Goodman 2005] is quoted here:

"The error can be positive or negative, depending on whether the actual wavefront lies to the left or to the right (respectively) of the Gaussian reference sphere."

The sign convension for the pathlength error is different is some other sources, such as in "Basic Wavefront Aberration Theory for Optical Metrology", by James C. Wyant and Katherine Creath, "Computational Fourier Optics", by Volez, Lecture notes on Aberrations by John E. Greivenkamp (OPTI-503, Optical Design and Instrumentation II).

Also, the relation between \(W_{020}\) and \(W_d\) is shown as follows:

\[W_{020} = \frac{W_d \, a^2}{2}\]

Rewriting the above equation in terms of \(W_d\) we have:

\[ h(u,v,z_i,z_o) = \frac{1}{{\lambda}^2z_o z_i} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int\limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} {P(x,y){e^{-jk \frac{W_d}{2} (x^2 + y^2)}} {e^{ - j\frac{k}{{{z_i}}}\left( {ux + vy} \right)}}dxdy} \]

Let,
\(x = \rho a cos\theta\)
\(y = \rho a sin\theta\)
such that,
\(0 \leq \rho \leq 1\)
\(x^2 + y^2 = \rho^2 a^2\)
 and
\(u = r cos \phi\)
\(v = r sin \phi\)

[Note that \(x\) and \(y\) are the pupil plane coordinates and \(u\) and \(v\) are the image plane coordinates. \(\theta\), \(\rho\) are radial pupil plane coordinates and \(r\), \(\phi\) are radial image plane coordinates.]

Then, in polar coordinates, we can re-write the above equation in the following form:

\[ h_0(r,\phi,z_i, z_o) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2z_o z_i} \int\limits_{\rho=0}^{1} \, \int\limits_{\theta=0}^{2\pi} { P(\rho, \theta) {e^{-j k \frac{W_d}{2} \, \rho^2 a^2 }} {e^{- j\frac{k\rho a r}{z_i} cos(\theta - \rho)}} {a^2} \rho d\rho d\theta } \]

Furthermore, if we have a circularly symmetric pupil, i.e. if \(P(\rho, \theta) = P_{g}(\rho)\), then the above equation reduces to

\[h_0(r,\phi,z_i,z_o) = \frac{ a^2}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} 2\pi \int\limits_{0}^{1} P_g(\rho) \left[ \frac{1}{2\pi} \int\limits_{0}^{2\pi}e^{-j\frac{k\rho a r}{z_i} cos(\theta -\phi)}d\theta \right] e^{-j k \frac{W_d}{2} \, \rho^2 a^2}\rho d \rho \]

Using the Bessel function identity (see Goodman, page 11, equation 2-30):

\[ J_0 (a) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int\limits_{0}^{2\pi} e^{-j a cos(\theta -\phi)} d\theta \]

we get

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \int\limits_{0}^{1} P_{g}(\rho) J_0\left(\frac{k\rho a r}{z_i} \right) e^{-j k \frac{W_d}{2} \,\rho^2 a^2}\rho d \rho \]

Note that the above expression is independent of \(\phi\) (meaning the function is rotationally symmetric).

[A little diversion ...]

For a circular aperture with uniform distribution across the pupil, when \(W_d = 0\) (i.e. when \(z_i = z_p\)), the above equation reduces to:

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2z_oz_i} \int\limits_{0}^{1} J_0\left(\frac{k\rho a r}{z_i} \right) \rho d \rho \]

In the above equation, a point object is located along the optical axis at a distance of \(z_o\) from the thin lens. The corresponding geometric point image is located on the optical axis at a distance of \(z_i\) on the other side of the thin lens, such that \(\frac{1}{z_o} + \frac{1}{z_i} = \frac{1}{f}\). \(r\) is the radial distance about the image point in the image plane.

Note that I have left the image distance (which is in focus) as \(z_i\) instead of using \(z_p\)

Let \(A = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2z_oz_i}\) and \(R = \frac{k a r}{z_i}\). Then,

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = A \int\limits_{0}^{1} J_0\left(R \rho\right) \rho d \rho \]

Substituting \(R\rho = \rho'\), the integration becomes

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = \frac{A}{R^2} \int\limits_{0}^{R} J_0\left(\rho'\right) \rho' d \rho' \]

Using the identity \(\int\limits_{0}^{x} \xi J_o(\xi) d \xi = xJ_1(x)\), the above integral becomes

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = A \frac{J_1 (R)}{R} \]

or

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2z_oz_i} \frac{J_1 (k a r / z_i)}{k a r / z_i} \]

The corresponding intensity distribution (after a little rearrangement) is given as:

\[I(r,z_i,z_o) = {\left(\frac{1}{\lambda z_o} \right)}^2 {\left(\frac{1}{\lambda z_i} \right)}^2 {\left[ 2\frac{J_1 (k a r / z_i)}{k a r / z_i} \right]}^2 \]

The above equation is the the Airy pattern with an extra scaling factor \({\left(\frac{1}{\lambda z_o} \right)}^2\), which represents the radiometric loss of light as it propagates a distance of \(z_o\) before the lens.

Thus the light distribution of the PSF in 3D can be written as:

\[h_0(r,z_i,z_o) = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2 z_i z_o} \int\limits_{0}^{1} P_{g}(\rho) J_0\left(\frac{k\rho a r}{z_i} \right) e^{-j k \frac{W_d}{2} \,\rho^2 a^2}\rho d \rho \]

In order to solve the above equation, we shall follow the path shown @ Born and Wolf in section 8.8.1, since the form of the equation is very similar to the expression in that book.

Let \(\tilde{u} = k W_d \, a^2\) and \(\tilde{v} = k\frac{a}{z_i}r\), we can write the above expression as:

\[ \boxed{ h_0(\tilde{v}, \tilde{u}, z_o) = \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \int\limits_{0}^{1} P_{g}(\rho) J_0\left( \tilde{v} \rho \right) e^{-j \tilde{u} \rho^2 /2}\rho d \rho } \]

Note:

The above expression is valid where paraxial assumptions are valid, and for a rotationally symmetric pupil.

In the above expression, \(\tilde{u}\) and \(\tilde{v}\) are dimensionless quantities in the image space (not in the pupil coordinate s[ace). Some other "forms" of \(\tilde{u}\) and \(\tilde{v}\) are:

\[ \boxed{ \tilde{u} = kW_da^2 = k\frac{a^2}{z_p (z_p + \delta z)} \delta z } \]

\[ \boxed{ \tilde{v} = k\frac{a}{z_i} \sqrt{u^2 + v^2} = k\frac{a}{(z_p + \delta z)} \sqrt{u^2 + v^2} } \]

In the above coordinate system, \(\delta z\) can be negitive or positive. It is negative for negative focal shift and positive for positive focal shift.

The above equation is similar to equation 12 of [Born and Wolf 2011] with two (related) differences:

  • In [Born and Wolf 2011] \(\tilde{u}\) is linearly related to \(\delta z\), the distance away from focus. In our case, \(\tilde{u}\) depends both on \(z_i\) and \(\delta z\).
  • In [Born and Wolf 2011] the term in the denominator of the argument of \(J_0\) is fixed (=\(f\)). In our case, it is \(z_i\).

Expressions for \(\tilde{u}\) and \(\tilde{v}\) in [Born & Wolf 2011]

In order to distinguish the expressions of \(\tilde{u}\) and \(\tilde{v}\) in [Born & Wolf 2011] and here, we shall assign a subscript (\(_{bw}\)) to denote [Born & Wolf 2011] expression.

\[ \tilde{u}_{bw} = \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}\left( \frac{a}{f} \right)^2 \delta z = \frac{k}{4 N^2} \delta z \]

\[ \tilde{v}_{bw} = \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}\left( \frac{a}{f} \right) r = \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda}\left( \frac{a}{f} \right) \sqrt{u^2 + v^2} = \frac{k}{2 N} \sqrt{u^2 + v^2} \]

where, \(N\) is the f-number.

Following the derivation similar to [Born and Wolf 2011], the intensity distribution in the vicinity of the focal point is given in terms of the Lommel functions:

The Lommel functions are given as:

\[U_n(\tilde{u}, \tilde{v}) = \sum_{s=0}^{\infty}{(-1)}s {\left(\frac{\tilde{u}}{\tilde{v}} \right)}^{n+2s} J_{n+2s}(\tilde{v}), \]

\[V_n(\tilde{u}, \tilde{v}) = \sum_{s=0}^{\infty}{(-1)}s {\left(\frac{\tilde{v}}{\tilde{u}} \right)}^{n+2s} J_{n+2s}(\tilde{v}) \]

Case 1: \(|\tilde{v}|\gt|\tilde{u}|\)

\[I(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) = {\left(\frac{2}{\tilde{u}}\right)}^2 \left[ U_1^2( \tilde{u},\tilde{v}) + U_2^2(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) \right] I_0 \]

Case 2: \(|\tilde{v}|\le|\tilde{u}|\)

\[I(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) = {\left(\frac{2}{\tilde{u}}\right)}^2 \left[ 1 + V_0^2(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) + V_1^2(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) - 2V_0(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) cos\left[\frac{1}{2}\left( \tilde{u} + \frac{\tilde{v}^2}{\tilde{u}} \right)\right] - 2V_1(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) sin\left[\frac{1}{2}\left( \tilde{u} + \frac{\tilde{v}^2}{\tilde{u}} \right)\right] \right] I_0 \]

Case 3: \(|\tilde{v}|=|\tilde{u}|\)

(using the identities of Jacobi)

\[I(\tilde{u},\tilde{v}) = \frac{ 1 - 2 J_0(\tilde{u})cos (\tilde{u}) + J_0^2(\tilde{u})}{\tilde{u}^2} I_0\]

where

\[I_0 = \left( \frac{2\pi a^2}{{\lambda}^2 z_o z_i} \right)^2 \] is the intensity at the geometrical focus \(\tilde{u}=\tilde{v}=0\).

It has been shown in [Mielenz 1998] that for computations of the Lommel functions to \(m\) decimals, the above expansions can be truncated when either of the conditions,

\[{\left(\frac{\tilde{v}}{\tilde{u}}\right)}^n \hspace{2 mm} or \hspace{2 mm} {\left(\frac{\tilde{u}}{\tilde{v}}\right)}^n \le \frac{1}{2}10^{-m}, \]

\[ n \geq \tilde{v} \hspace{2 mm} and \hspace{2 mm} J_n(\tilde{v}) \le \frac{1}{2}10^{-m} \]

are satisfied.
